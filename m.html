Mathcloud Tutorial - Matter and Its Properties

1. States of Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. The three main states of matter are solid, liquid, and gas, each with distinct characteristics.

Solid

  • Shape: Definite, does not change unless force is applied.
  • Volume: Definite, the same regardless of container size.
  • Particle Arrangement: Particles are tightly packed and arranged in a regular pattern. They vibrate but do not move freely.
  • Movement: Only vibrate in place (no flow).
  • Examples: Ice, metal, wood.
Particles in a solid (ice).

Liquid

  • Shape: Indefinite, takes the shape of the container.
  • Volume: Definite, does not change, regardless of container.
  • Particle Arrangement: Particles are close together but can slide past one another, allowing the liquid to flow.
  • Movement: Particles can move and flow, allowing liquids to change shape.
  • Examples: Water, oil, juice.
Particles in a liquid (water).

Gas

  • Shape: Indefinite, expands to fill the container.
  • Volume: Indefinite, the volume changes depending on the size of the container.
  • Particle Arrangement: Particles are far apart and move freely at high speeds.
  • Movement: Particles move freely and quickly in all directions.
  • Examples: Air, oxygen, carbon dioxide.
Particles in a gas (air).

Plasma (Bonus State)

  • Shape & Volume: Indefinite.
  • Particles: Very high-energy particles, some of which are charged (ionized).
  • Examples: The sun, lightning, neon lights.

2. Changes of State

Matter can change from one state to another by either gaining or losing energy (in the form of heat). These changes are called physical changes because the substance remains the same, only its state changes.

Melting (Solid → Liquid)

  • Description: The process where a solid turns into a liquid.
  • Temperature: Happens at the melting point (e.g., 0°C for ice).
  • Energy: Heat energy is absorbed by the solid, causing the particles to vibrate more and eventually break free from their fixed positions.
Watch how ice melts into water.

Freezing (Liquid → Solid)

  • Description: The process where a liquid turns into a solid.
  • Temperature: Happens at the freezing point (e.g., 0°C for water).
  • Energy: Heat energy is released by the liquid, causing the particles to slow down and form a fixed structure.

Boiling (Liquid → Gas)

  • Description: The process where a liquid turns into a gas at the boiling point.
  • Temperature: Happens at the boiling point (e.g., 100°C for water).
  • Energy: Heat energy is absorbed, causing the particles to move faster and overcome the forces holding them together, turning the liquid into vapor.
See the boiling process of water.

Condensation (Gas → Liquid)

  • Description: The process where a gas turns into a liquid.
  • Temperature: Happens when the gas cools and loses heat.
  • Energy: Heat energy is released by the gas, causing the particles to slow down and form a liquid.

Sublimation (Solid → Gas without liquid phase)

  • Description: The process where a solid turns directly into a gas without becoming a liquid.
  • Example: Dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) turning into gas.
Sublimation of dry ice.

Deposition (Gas → Solid without liquid phase)

  • Description: The process where a gas turns directly into a solid without becoming a liquid.
  • Example: Frost forming from water vapor on a cold surface.
Deposition of frost from water vapor.
